Appam with Stew

Description

Appam with Stew is arguably one of the most famous and beloved traditional dishes in Kerala. Appam is a type of pancake made from fermented rice batter and coconut milk. The edges are delightfully crisp, while the center remains soft, fluffy, and slightly sweet. It is almost always paired with Ishtu or Stew, which can be made with vegetables, chicken, or mutton. The vegetable stew is a creamy, aromatic curry made with potatoes, carrots, green peas, and beans, all gently simmered in a rich coconut milk base.

The magic of this dish lies in its subtle flavors. The stew is not overly spicy; instead, it relies on the fragrance of whole spices like cloves, cardamom, cinnamon, and the soothing texture of thick coconut milk. The fermentation process for the appam traditionally involves using 'toddy' (palm wine), though yeast is commonly used today. The combination of the hot, laced pancake soaking up the mildly spiced, creamy coconut stew is a match made in heaven. It is a staple breakfast item in Kerala homes and is also prominently featured in festive feasts like Christmas and Easter.

Benefits

  • Gut Health: The fermented rice batter in appam is rich in probiotics.
  • Nutritious Vegetables: The stew is loaded with vegetables providing essential vitamins and minerals.
  • Healthy Fats: Coconut milk provides medium-chain triglycerides, which are good for heart health.
  • Gluten-Free: Appam is naturally gluten-free, making it an excellent choice for those with gluten intolerance.

Instructions

For Appam: Soak raw rice for a few hours and grind it to a smooth paste with grated coconut, cooked rice, and a little yeast. Let the batter ferment overnight (about 8-10 hours). Add a little sugar and salt before cooking. Pour a ladleful of batter into an Appachatti (a curved pan), swirl to coat the sides, cover, and cook until the center is set and edges are brown.

For Stew: Heat coconut oil, sauté whole spices (cinnamon, cloves, cardamom), sliced onions, ginger, and green chilies. Add chopped vegetables like potatoes and carrots, pour thin coconut milk, and cook until the vegetables are tender. Finish by adding thick coconut milk, a few curry leaves, and a drizzle of raw coconut oil. Do not boil after adding thick coconut milk.
